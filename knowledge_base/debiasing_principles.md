# Debiasing Principles

Recruitment evaluations can be distorted by cognitive biases.

Affinity bias occurs when evaluators favor candidates who seem similar to themselves or familiar social groups. To reduce affinity bias, focus only on explicit job-related criteria.

Conformity bias occurs when evaluators rely on assumptions or social expectations rather than evidence. To reduce conformity bias, check whether each claim is supported by the resume or job description.

Role congruity bias occurs when evaluators expect certain demographic groups to fit or not fit particular roles. To reduce role congruity bias, evaluate demonstrated skills and experience rather than demographic stereotypes.

Age bias occurs when evaluators infer ability, adaptability, ambition, or reliability from age-related cues. To reduce age bias, ignore age cues and assess only role-relevant skills, experience, and qualifications.

Name-based ethnic bias occurs when evaluators infer language ability, cultural fit, nationality, or competence from a candidate name. To reduce name-based bias, do not use name familiarity or ethnicity-associated cues in the evaluation.

Gender bias occurs when evaluators infer leadership ability, technical competence, communication style, or commitment from gender cues. To reduce gender bias, assess only evidence explicitly present in the resume.

Educational-prestige bias occurs when evaluators infer competence from the perceived status or familiarity of an institution rather than from role-relevant evidence. To reduce this bias, evaluate documented skills, experience, and training rather than school prestige unless a specific qualification is required by the job.

Career-gap or non-linear-path bias occurs when evaluators infer lower motivation, reliability, or competence from incomplete or non-linear work histories. To reduce this bias, state evidence gaps neutrally and avoid unsupported assumptions about reasons for gaps.

Before finalizing the evaluation, verify that the explanation does not rely on demographic information, cultural-fit assumptions, or unsupported inferences. If evidence is missing, explicitly mark it as missing rather than converting uncertainty into a negative judgment.
